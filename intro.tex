Diffusion models \cite{ho2020denoising,
song2020denoising,
dhariwal2021diffusionmodelsbeatgans,
pmlr-v37-sohl-dickstein15,
song2019generative,
rombach2021highresolution}
have achieved state-of-the-art performance
and garnered considerable attention
as leading methodologies
in the field of generative modeling,
particularly excelling in image \cite{saharia2022photorealistic,chen2023pixartalpha,podell2023sdxlimprovinglatentdiffusion},
and video \cite{videoworldsimulators2024,ma2025latte} synthesis tasks.
To further scale model capacity and improve generation quality,
recent research has shifted the architectural paradigm
from convolutional U-Net backbones \cite{ronneberger2015unetconvolutionalnetworksbiomedical,ho2020denoising}
to Diffusion Transformers (DiT) \cite{Peebles2022DiT,bao2022all},
which substantially enhance modeling capability.

Despite the state-of-the-art performance,
this architectural advancement
introduces elevated computational cost
resulting in substantially prolonged inference latency.
The computational burden
is further exacerbated
by the sequential and multi-step iterative sampling procedure
characteristic of diffusion models \cite{shih2023paradigms},
wherein each sampling step
necessitates a complete forward pass through the network.

Based on the observation that consecutive diffusion steps
exhibit feature similarities,
feature reuse methods \cite{
    Xu_2018,
    wimbauer2024cachecanacceleratingdiffusion,
    selvaraju2024forafastforwardcachingdiffusion,
    zou2024accelerating,
    zou2025rethinkingtokenwisefeaturecaching}
have been proposed to accelerate generation
by strategically caching
and reusing intermediate activations
across successive denoising iterations.

However,
existing cache-based methods
often rely on static reuse strategies
or hand-tuned similarity thresholds,
which are misaligned with the non-uniform difficulty
of diffusion timesteps.
In practice,
the amount of inter-step change
varies across the denoising trajectory:
aggressive reuse can accumulate drift and erase fine details,
while conservative policies
leave substantial redundancy unexploited.
This motivates a predictive, confidence-aware mechanism
that can adapt reuse decisions online,
skipping computation when dynamics are stable
and falling back when they are not.
We investigate the activation patterns across consecutive denoising steps
by measuring cosine similarity
and relative change
between consecutive transformer outputs:
\begin{equation}
    \begin{aligned}
        s_k &=
        \frac{\langle \mathbf{y}_{k-1}, \mathbf{y}_k \rangle}
        {\|\mathbf{y}_{k-1}\|_2 \|\mathbf{y}_k\|_2}, \\
        \delta_k &=
        \frac{\|\mathbf{y}_k - \mathbf{y}_{k-1}\|_2}
        {\|\mathbf{y}_{k-1}\|_2}.
    \end{aligned}
    \label{eq:intro:step-similarity}
\end{equation}
As shown in \Cref{fig:motivation-latent-similarity},
most intermediate steps exhibit high cosine similarity
and low relative change with tight inter-quantile bands,
showing that adjacent transformer outputs remain highly aligned
throughout the denoising process.
Taken together,
these trends suggest that,
over short temporal windows,
the denoising trajectory is not only strongly coherent
but also evolves in an approximately linear manner
in feature space.
This observation naturally motivates
Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) \cite{kutz2016dynamic,dey2022dynamicmodedecompositionkoopman},
which approximates a dynamical system
with a local linear evolution operator
estimated directly from a sequence of snapshots.

This perspective is particularly appealing
when compared with existing cache forecasting strategies.
TaylorSeer \cite{TaylorSeer2025}
and HiCache \cite{feng2025hicachetrainingfreeaccelerationdiffusion}
predict future features
through fixed polynomial bases,
such as Taylor or Hermite expansions,
whose extrapolation behavior is tied
to a prescribed basis and expansion order.
TeaCache \cite{liu2024timestep}
and MagCache \cite{ma2025magcache},
in contrast,
rely on predesigned rescaling functions,
calibrated magnitude ratios,
and hand-tuned thresholds
to determine when cached outputs should be reused.
By comparison,
DMD estimates its evolution operator online
from the recent activation history itself,
so the effective extrapolation coefficients
are adapted to the observed local dynamics
rather than fixed in advance, which makes 
DMD better suited
to non-uniform timestep spacing,
where the difficulty of prediction
can vary substantially along the denoising trajectory.


Based on the DMD predictor,
we estimate the next-step activation
and compare it with the subsequent exact transformer output
through the prediction error:
\begin{equation}
    e_k =
    \frac{\|\hat{\mathbf{y}}_k - \mathbf{y}_k\|_2}
    {\|\mathbf{y}_k\|_2},
    \label{eq:intro:prediction-error}
\end{equation}
Cached outputs are reused only when:
\begin{equation}
    e_k < \tau.
    \label{eq:intro:reuse-gate}
\end{equation}
This ties the skip decision directly
to observed forecasting accuracy.

Motivated by this insight,
we propose $D^3$-Cache,
a dynamic caching strategy
that leverages DMD-based modal analysis
to predict transformer outputs online
and gate cache reuse with the observed prediction error,
thereby enabling selective skipping of expensive forward passes
without compromising generation fidelity.


\quad In summary,
our contributions are as follows:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=2pt, topsep=0pt, parsep=0pt]
\item \textbf{Observation of Strong Inter-Step Structure:}
We show that transformer outputs across consecutive diffusion steps
exhibit high cosine similarity and low relative change,
revealing strong temporal coherence throughout the denoising process.
Moreover, for most intermediate steps,
the output trajectory is well approximated by a locally linear evolution,
which motivates linear dynamical modeling for cache forecasting.

\item \textbf{$D^3$-Cache:}
We introduce $D^3$-Cache,
a training-free diffusion acceleration framework
that uses Dynamic Mode Decomposition
to model the short-horizon evolution
of diffusion transformer outputs
from only a small number of recent activation snapshots.
Combined with prediction-error-based gating,
$D^3$-Cache provides a lightweight
and efficient cache reuse strategy.


\item \textbf{State-of-the-Art Performance of $D^3$-Cache:}
Comprehensive experiments on state-of-the-art diffusion models
(FLUX.1 \cite{flux2024}, HiDream-I1-full \cite{hidreami1technicalreport}, Wan2.1 \cite{wan2025}, and HunyuanVideo \cite{kong2024hunyuanvideo})
demonstrate that $D^3$-Cache achieves over 2.5x computational acceleration consistently.
Compared with existing cache-based acceleration methods at iso-speedup conditions,
$D^3$-Cache significantly outperforms in generation fidelity,
yielding superior visual quality for both image and video synthesis.
\end{itemize}
