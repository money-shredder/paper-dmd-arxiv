\section{Method}\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Preliminaries}\label{sub:method:preliminaries}

\textbf{Diffusion model basics.}
Diffusion models formulate a forward noising process
over $T$ timesteps for data $\mathbf{x}_0 \sim p_{\text{data}}$
as
\begin{equation}
    q(\mathbf{x}_t \mid \mathbf{x}_{t-1}) = \mathcal{N}\!
    \left(\mathbf{x}_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t}\mathbf{x}_{t-1}, \beta_t \mathbf{I}\right),
    \label{eq:method:prelim:forward}
\end{equation}
where $\{\beta_t\}$ is a predefined variance schedule.
This admits a closed-form solution
\begin{equation}
    \mathbf{x}_t =
    \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t}\mathbf{x}_0 +
    \sqrt{1-\bar{\alpha}_t}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t, \quad
    \boldsymbol{\epsilon}_t \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I}),
    \label{eq:method:prelim:forward-closed}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{\alpha}_t = \prod_{s=1}^t (1-\beta_s)$.
The reverse process is characterized by
\begin{equation}
    p_\theta(\mathbf{x}_{t-1} \mid \mathbf{x}_t, \mathbf{c}) = \mathcal{N}\!
    \left(\mathbf{x}_{t-1};
    \boldsymbol{\mu}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t, \mathbf{c}),
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_t, t)\right),
    \label{eq:method:prelim:reverse}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{c}$ denotes the conditioning information.
Sampling begins from $\mathbf{x}_T \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$,
iteratively denoising toward $\mathbf{x}_0$.

\textbf{Dynamic Mode Decomposition.}
Dynamic Mode Decomposition (DMD) models the local temporal evolution
of a sequence of snapshots
$\{\mathbf{y}_i\}_{i=1}^m \subset \mathbb{R}^D$
by a linear operator $\mathbf{A} \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times D}$
such that $\mathbf{y}_{i+1} \approx \mathbf{A}\mathbf{y}_i$,
or equivalently $\mathbf{Y} \approx \mathbf{A}\mathbf{X}$.
Here, $m$ denotes the number of snapshots in the local history window
and $D$ denotes the dimension of each snapshot vector.
In \Cref{sub:method:dmd-cache},
each $\mathbf{y}_i$ corresponds to a flattened transformer output
at one denoising step.
Given snapshots $\{\mathbf{y}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{y}_m\}$,
we form the shifted snapshot matrices
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
    \mathbf{X} &= [\mathbf{y}_{1}, \ldots, \mathbf{y}_{m-1}]
    \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times (m-1)}, \\
    \mathbf{Y} &= [\mathbf{y}_{2}, \ldots, \mathbf{y}_{m}]
    \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times (m-1)},
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:method:xy}
\end{equation}
and obtain the least-squares approximation
$\mathbf{A} \approx \mathbf{Y}\mathbf{X}^{\dagger}$,
where $(\cdot)^{\dagger}$ denotes the Moore--Penrose pseudoinverse.
To compute this efficiently,
we first take the compact singular value decomposition of $\mathbf{X}$,
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{X} = \mathbf{U}_r \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r \mathbf{V}_r^{\top},
\label{eq:method:svd}
\end{equation}
where
$\mathbf{U}_r \in \mathbb{R}^{D \times r}$,
$\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times r}$,
$\mathbf{V}_r \in \mathbb{R}^{(m-1) \times r}$,
and $r = \min(D, m-1)$.
The reduced operator, its eigendecomposition,
and the corresponding DMD modes are then
\begin{equation}
\begin{gathered}
\tilde{\mathbf{A}} = \mathbf{U}_r^{\top}\mathbf{Y}\mathbf{V}_r\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r^{-1}, \\
\tilde{\mathbf{A}}\mathbf{W} = \mathbf{W}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}, \\
\mathbf{\Phi} = \mathbf{Y}\mathbf{V}_r\boldsymbol{\Sigma}_r^{-1}\mathbf{W}.
\end{gathered}
\label{eq:method:dmd-core}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{W} \in \mathbb{C}^{r \times r}$ contains the eigenvectors,
$\boldsymbol{\Lambda} \in \mathbb{C}^{r \times r}$ is the diagonal matrix
of eigenvalues, and $\mathbf{\Phi} \in \mathbb{C}^{D \times r}$
collects the DMD modes.
The modal coefficients associated with the latest snapshot are
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{\Phi}^{\dagger}\mathbf{y}_m,
\label{eq:method:coeff}
\end{equation}
which gives the one-step prediction
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{m+1}
= \operatorname{Re}\!\left(\mathbf{\Phi}\boldsymbol{\Lambda}\mathbf{b}\right),
\label{eq:method:dmd:predict-discrete}
\end{equation}
where $\operatorname{Re}(\cdot)$ denotes the real part.

\subsection{DMD for Diffusion Models}\label{sub:method:dmd-cache}
$\mathrm{D}^3$-Cache treats the sequence of diffusion transformer output tensors $\{\mathbf{y}_k\}$
as a temporal signal
and applies DMD to learn the local linear dynamics
over short temporal windows.
This yields a data-driven predictor
for subsequent transformer outputs
and enables selective skipping of expensive forward passes
when recent preview errors indicate reliable extrapolation.

Diffusion inference proceeds as a discrete-time denoising trajectory
across timesteps $\{t_k\}_{k=1}^K$.
The interval between two consecutive steps is
\begin{equation}
\Delta t_k = |t_k - t_{k-1}|,
\label{eq:method:dt}
\end{equation}
which may vary across different inference schedules.
At step $k$,
the wrapped diffusion transformer produces a primary output tensor
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{y}_k = f_\theta(\mathbf{z}_k, t_k, \mathbf{c}),
\label{eq:method:prelim:diffusion}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{z}_k$ is the current latent state,
$\mathbf{c}$ is the conditioning signal (e.g., text embedding),
$\theta$ are the model parameters,
and each sample in the output tensor is flattened into a vector in $\mathbb{R}^D$
before DMD fitting.
In implementation,
$\mathbf{y}_k$ is the primary tensor extracted from the module return value:
the tensor itself,
the \texttt{sample} field,
or the first element of a tuple/list output.
Unlike layer-wise caching methods that cache intermediate activations
$\{\mathbf{h}_k^{(\ell)}\}_{\ell=1}^{L}$ from individual blocks,
$\mathrm{D}^3$-Cache operates in the \emph{transformer-output space},
caching only the primary output tensor $\mathbf{y}_k$.
This design reduces the memory footprint relative to storing layer-wise features,
remains directly applicable across architectures with different block structures,
and aligns naturally with the diffusion sampling process.
We therefore maintain a sliding history window
containing up to the most recent $m$ exact outputs
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{H}_k = \{\mathbf{y}_{\tau_1}, \mathbf{y}_{\tau_2}, \ldots, \mathbf{y}_{\tau_n}\},
\label{eq:method:history}
\end{equation}
where $\tau_1 < \tau_2 < \cdots < \tau_n \le k$ index the denoising steps
at which full forward passes were executed, and $n \le m$.
Alongside $\mathcal{H}_k$,
we maintain a companion buffer $\mathcal{D}_k$
containing up to the most recent $m$ positive timestep intervals,
and estimate their mean as
\begin{equation}
\bar{\Delta t}
= \frac{1}{|\mathcal{D}_k|} \sum_{\Delta t \in \mathcal{D}_k} \Delta t,
\label{eq:method:avg-dt}
\end{equation}
which is the quantity used in continuous-time extrapolation.
The history is updated only when a full forward pass is executed.
Cached predictions are returned to the sampler
but are not inserted into $\mathcal{H}_k$,
and the number of consecutive cached steps is capped
to limit error accumulation.

Given the history window $\mathcal{H}_k$,
we construct the DMD snapshot matrices $(\mathbf{X}, \mathbf{Y})$
according to \Cref{eq:method:xy}
and compute the corresponding DMD quantities
$(\mathbf{\Phi}, \boldsymbol{\Lambda}, \mathbf{b})$
following the procedure described in \Cref{sub:method:preliminaries}.
To accommodate non-uniform timestep spacing,
we transform the discrete-time eigenvalues
into continuous-time frequencies via
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\omega}
= \frac{1}{\bar{\Delta t}}
\log\left(\operatorname{diag}(\boldsymbol{\Lambda})\right),
\label{eq:method:omega}
\end{equation}
where $\log(\cdot)$ is applied element-wise.
The transformer output at the subsequent denoising step
is then predicted as
\begin{equation}
\hat{\mathbf{y}}_{k+1}(\Delta t_{k+1})
= \operatorname{Re}\!\left(
    \mathbf{\Phi}
    \left(
        \exp(\boldsymbol{\omega} \Delta t_{k+1})
        \odot \mathbf{b}
    \right)
\right),
\label{eq:method:predict}
\end{equation}
Under uniform timestep spacing,
this expression reduces to the standard discrete-time DMD predictor
in \Cref{eq:method:dmd:predict-discrete}.
The predicted output is returned to the sampler
only when the adaptive gating policy in
\Cref{sub:method:adaptive_caching} permits reuse;
otherwise, an exact forward pass is performed,
the preview error is updated when available,
and the history window is refreshed with the resulting exact output.

\subsection{Adaptive Caching Policy}\label{sub:method:adaptive_caching}

In addition to the history buffer,
the cache maintains the most recent preview error
computed from an exact forward pass.
Whenever a valid timestep interval is available,
we first form a \emph{preview} prediction
$\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k+1}$
from the current history
without consuming the skip budget.
After the exact transformer output $\mathbf{y}_{k+1}$ is obtained,
the gate signal is updated using the relative $\ell_2$ error
\begin{equation}
e_{k+1}
=
\frac{
    \left\|
        \mathrm{vec}\!\left(\tilde{\mathbf{y}}_{k+1}\right)
        -
        \mathrm{vec}\!\left(\mathbf{y}_{k+1}\right)
    \right\|_2
}{
    \left\|
        \mathrm{vec}\!\left(\mathbf{y}_{k+1}\right)
    \right\|_2
},
\label{eq:method:gate-error}
\end{equation}
where $\mathrm{vec}(\cdot)$ flattens the transformer output
into a vector in $\mathbb{R}^D$.
This design ties the gate signal directly
to the empirical prediction accuracy
of the DMD extrapolator.

The reuse decision is governed by two hyperparameters:
$S_{\max}$ is the maximum consecutive cached steps,
which limits error accumulation and long-range drift; and
$\delta_g$ is the gate threshold applied to the most recent preview error.
$s_k$ is the current skip counter tracking consecutive cached steps
since the last full computation.
At step \(k+1\),
cache reuse is permitted only when both conditions are satisfied:
\begin{equation}
\begin{aligned}
e_k &\le \delta_g, \\
s_k &< S_{\max}.
\end{aligned}
\label{eq:method:gating}
\end{equation}
Otherwise, an exact forward pass is performed,
the preview error is updated according to \Cref{eq:method:gate-error},
and the resulting exact output is inserted into the history buffer.
